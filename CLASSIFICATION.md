You're putting your finger on the right constraint, and it's worth stating precisely: the limiting factor isn't *breadth*, it's *depth-as-judgment*. Breadth is a retrieval problem, and retrieval is the part AI handles well — give it the full model as a reference and RAG will recall which object exists, what a field means, and which targets a given source column could plausibly map to, across hundreds of objects and far more fields. What RAG does *not* do is make the modeling decision. Recall and modeling are different problems, and only the first is closed by feeding in the complete schema.

Let me sharpen one thing in your framing, because it actually reinforces your point. The strategic lever you're naming is real, but the precise three are the **Data Object Type Categories — Profile, Engagement, and Other**; Party is the main *subject area* that lives inside the Profile world (Individual, Account, Contact Point, Party Identification). The reason category is "the most important" decision, and the reason it resists automation, is that it's nearly irreversible: a DMO inherits its category from the *first* DLO mapped to it, and from then on only same-category DLOs can map to it. So it's an architectural commitment made at mapping time whose cost shows up much later. And the inputs you need to make it correctly — is this profile data we'll unify on, engagement data we'll segment on, or reference data — aren't in the schema. They're in the downstream intent: identity resolution, segmentation, activation. A model reading the schema can't see those because they don't exist yet at modeling time.

That's the precise boundary of why it's "not fully resolvable." The residue after RAG isn't a knowledge gap — it's judgment under consequence:

The category assignment above. Semantic mapping ambiguity, where one source field has several plausible standard-DMO targets and choosing correctly requires business meaning rather than a name or type match. Which fields become identity-resolution match keys and what the reconciliation rules are — that depends on your actual data quality and your tolerance for false merges, not on the model. Custom-versus-standard extension choices and the interlocking dependencies they create. And underneath all of it, accountability: these are governance, privacy, and cost decisions, and a model can propose them but can't *own* them.

So I'd frame the division of labor like this. RAG over the complete model genuinely promotes the AI from "recalls the main objects" to "proposes complete, grounded mappings with the full field space in view" — and that's a real lift. It's excellent at the high-recall, high-tedium parts: field discovery, candidate mappings, coverage and consistency checks, surfacing the fields a given feature requires, flagging what's left unmapped. But the data architect still owns the categorization strategy, the match keys, the semantic disambiguation, and the governance call. The AI makes that person faster and more exhaustive; it doesn't make them optional.

The part I'd push back on slightly is any implication that this is a *temporary* limit that more context or a better model dissolves. It isn't, and that's the interesting part. Even a hypothetical perfect-recall model over the entire schema still can't skip the judgment, because the judgment's inputs — business intent, downstream use, risk tolerance, who's accountable — live outside the document you'd feed it. RAG closes the recall gap completely and still leaves that, which is exactly the shape you described. It's the same line from a few turns back: AI drafts the specification and compresses weeks of blank-page work; the human signs the parts that carry consequences.
