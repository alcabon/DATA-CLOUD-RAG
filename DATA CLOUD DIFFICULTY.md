The honest framing first: difficulty for me doesn't track how intellectually hard a task is. It tracks three other things — how much the task depends on signal I can't see (real data quality, business intent), how *verifiable* my output is, and how *consequential and irreversible* a wrong call is. On that axis, hardest first:

**1. Identity resolution (match and reconciliation rules).** The hardest, and it isn't close. The right ruleset depends on the actual data — fill rates, format consistency, duplicate patterns — which I can't see without profiling, so even my proposals are weak guesses until someone runs the numbers. The failure modes are severe and asymmetric: a false merge blends two real people's data into one profile (a privacy incident), a false split fragments someone, and neither is visible from reading the ruleset. Thresholds are tuned empirically and iteratively, and I can't verify my own output. That combination — likely to be plausible-but-wrong *and* hard to catch — is the worst possible profile for me. I can propose candidate keys; I should never be the one who decides them.

**2. Segmentation.** Writing the segment logic is easy; knowing whether it captures the intended audience is not. Correctness here is defined relative to a business objective that lives outside the data, and validated against the real population distribution and downstream activation results — none of which I see at authoring time. There's no static ground truth to check against. I can scaffold the logic; I can't judge fit.

**3. Category classification (Profile / Engagement / Other).** Intellectually simple, structurally hard for me to own — for the reasons from a few turns back. It's driven by downstream intent that isn't in the schema, it's near-irreversible once the first DLO maps, and the consequences are delayed, so there's nothing to verify at decision time. (PII/sensitivity classification is the softer cousin: pattern-based, so I'm a decent flagger, but high enough consequence that it still needs human sign-off.)

**4. Calculated Insights.** Medium, and much safer. The aggregation logic sits squarely in reach, and crucially it's *checkable* — you can validate both the definition and the resulting numbers. The only real judgment is the metric definition itself (LTV measured how, over what window), which is a business agreement rather than a capability gap. Verifiable and reversible.

**5. Mapping.** The most AI-assistable of the set, even though it's where most of the volume is. Candidate generation is exactly my strength — high recall over the model. The residue is semantic disambiguation when several DMO fields plausibly fit, which produces confident-but-wrong matches — but those are cheap to catch in a review table and cheap to remap. Checkable plus reversible means low risk even when I'm wrong.

**6. Transformation, standardization, formula logic.** Easiest, because it's nearly code: well-specified, runnable, verifiable, reversible. The only real risk is edge cases in real data I haven't seen, and those are testable.

The pattern worth noticing is that my difficulty curve is *inverted* relative to mechanical effort. The parts a junior analyst finds laborious — writing CI queries, segment SQL, transformation rules — are easy for me. The parts a senior architect earns their seat on — identity-resolution thresholds, category strategy, segment fit — are precisely where I'm weakest. Cleverness of logic isn't the variable; unverifiability, dependence on real-data signal, and stakes are.

So the practical placement is clean: lean on me where the output is checkable and reversible (mapping, transformations, CI drafts, and candidate-everything), and keep the architect firmly on the empirical, low-verifiability, high-stakes calls. My one systematic failure mode is the same across all six — fluent, well-formed output that happens to be wrong — and it's only dangerous where it's hard to detect. Identity resolution is where it's both most likely and least detectable, which is why it's the one I'd never let myself decide.
